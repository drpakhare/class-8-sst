Chapter 2 · Tapestry of the Past

Reshaping India's Political Map

From the 13th to the 17th centuries — invasions, sultanates, empires that rose and fell, and the many kingdoms that resisted. A story of destruction and resilience.

Big Idea The one-line story

Between the 11th and 17th centuries (the later "medieval" period), waves of invaders from beyond the Hindu Kush — mostly Turkic and Afghan — reshaped India's map. The Delhi Sultanate (5 dynasties) rose and shrank; the Vijayanagara Empire flourished in the south; the Mughals built a mighty empire. Everywhere, regional powers — Rajputs, Ahoms, Sikhs and others — resisted. Politically unstable, but economically prosperous, India survived through the sword and through fresh creation in art and spirit.

What "medieval" means here: the term came from European history; for this book it simply means the 11th to 17th centuries in India.

Key Concept 1 The Delhi Sultanate — five dynasties

Formed after the defeat of Prithviraj Chauhan (1192), the Sultanate saw five successive Turkic-Afghan dynasties:

Mamluks (Slave) → Khiljis → Tughlaqs → Sayyids → Lodis
Memory hook: "My Kind Teacher Says Learn" = Mamluk, Khilji, Tughlaq, Sayyid, Lodi.
  • Instability: nearly 2 out of 3 sultans seized power by killing their predecessor — average reign was barely 9 years.
  • Ala-ud-din Khilji: raided north & central India, repelled Mongol invasions, called himself "the second Alexander" (Sikander Sani). His general Malik Kafur pushed south.
  • Muhammad bin Tughlaq: for the first time since the Mauryas most of the subcontinent came under one ruler. Two failed schemes: shifting the capital to Daulatabad (people forced to walk 1,000+ km) and token currency (cheap copper coins declared equal to silver/gold → counterfeiting → economic decline).
  • Timur (1398): a brutal Turkic-Mongol conqueror sacked Delhi and left with huge plunder. Afterwards the Lodis — the last Sultanate dynasty — emerged, but the territory had shrunk.
Def →Jizya = a tax imposed on non-Muslim subjects; Iconoclasm = destruction of religious images.

Key Concept 3 The Vijayanagara Empire (est. 1336)

  • Founded by brothers Harihara and Bukka at Hampi (guided by guru Vidyaranya) after they rejected Delhi's authority.
  • Rival to the north: the Bahmani Sultanate, which later split into the five Deccan Sultanates — Bijapur, Golconda, Berar, Ahmednagar, Bidar.
  • Krishnadevaraya (16th c.) = greatest ruler; military power + cultural renaissance. Wrote the Telugu epic Āmuktamālyada; patronised Sanskrit, Telugu, Kannada; granted temples (Tirupati, Vitthala).
  • Domingo Paes, a Portuguese traveller, described the city as "as large as Rome... the best provided city in the world."
  • 1565 — Battle of Talikota: the Deccan Sultanates' coalition defeated Vijayanagara (led by Ramaraya); the city was sacked and ruined. The empire fragmented under the Nayakas.
"Pati" titles: Vijayanagara kings = Narapati (lord of men), Bahmani = Ashwapati (lord of horses), Marathas = Chhatrapati (lord of the parasol/crown).

Def →Jauhar = Rajput women's act of self-immolation to avoid capture (e.g. at Chittorgarh under Akbar).

Def →Guerrilla warfare = small groups using terrain knowledge for surprise attacks to beat bigger armies (used by Maharana Pratap & the Ahoms).
Def →Paik system (Ahoms) = every able-bodied man gave labour/military service in exchange for land — a large force without a permanent army.

Key Concept 7 People's Lives — the economy

  • Despite instability, India stayed one of the world's wealthiest regions — agrarian base, artisan industries, temple economies, wide trade.
  • Agriculture = mainstay; land revenue usually one-fifth of produce (some sultans took up to one-half).
  • Craft & trade: textiles, ship-building; exports through Calicut, Surat, Masulipatnam, Hooghly, Mangalore. India exported more than it imported.
  • Śhreṇis = guilds; jātis = professional communities. Hundi = a written payment instruction (early banking) letting merchants move money without carrying cash. Marwaris traded across regimes.
  • Temples were economic hubs — markets, credit, dāna (donations), dharmaśhālās & chhatrams for pilgrims.
  • But: late 1600s saw stress — peasants kept a small share after taxes; many became bonded labourers; famines depended on the ruler's benevolence.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. "Why did kingdoms like Vijayanagara and the Ahoms resist conquest longer than others?" [3]
Geo:Difficult terrain — hills, forests and rivers (Aravallis, Brahmaputra Valley) — made invasion hard.
Military:Smart tactics: Ahoms used guerrilla warfare & the paik system; Vijayanagara had strong armies and forts.
Social:Strong leadership & unity (Lachit Borphukan; Harihara–Bukka) and local support.
∴ Geography + tactics + united leadership let them hold out far longer.
Example B. "How did Akbar change from a ruthless conqueror to a tolerant ruler?" [5]
Early:As a young king he was brutal — the 5-month siege and massacre at Chittor (~30,000 civilians).
Shift:As he grew in knowledge he felt it wrong to force his faith on others ("what constancy from proselytes on compulsion?").
Policies:Abolished jizya, promoted sulh-i-kul, Rajput marriage alliances, Hindu officials, interfaith dialogues, "house of translation."
Result:A more stable, expanded empire with wide support, even from many Rajputs.
∴ Experience + strategy turned military might into tolerance-based statecraft.

Traps & Quick Recall Don't mix these up

Panipat confusion: 1st Panipat (1526) = Babur vs Ibrahim Lodi (Mughal Empire begins). 2nd Panipat (1556) = Akbar (via Bairam Khan) vs Hemu.
Talikota (1565) destroyed Vijayanagara. Haldighati (1576) = Maharana Pratap. Saraighat (1671) = Ahoms win.
  • Konark Sun temple = Narasimhadeva I (Eastern Gangas). Belur/Halebidu = Hoysalas. Kumbhalgarh = Rana Kumbha.
  • Iqta (Sultanate) vs Mansabdari (Akbar). Todar Mal = revenue. Abul Fazl = Ain-i-Akbari.
  • Ik Onkār = one God. Khalsa = 1699, Guru Gobind Singh.
