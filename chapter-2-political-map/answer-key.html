Section B Short Answer

Q11
Mamluks (Slave dynasty) → Khiljis → Tughlaqs → Sayyids → Lodis.
Q12
Paik system: every able-bodied man gave labour or military service to the state in exchange for land rights. It let the ruler build infrastructure and keep a large force without a permanent standing army.
Q13
A hundi was a written instruction to make a payment. It could be carried across political borders, so merchants transferred funds without physically carrying currency — safer from plunder (an early form of banking).

Section C Long Answer

Q14
Iqta (Delhi Sultanate): land assigned to nobles (iqtadars) to collect taxes for the Sultan; posts not hereditary. Mansabdari (Akbar): officers ranked by mansab, kept a set number of troops/horses, paid via land (jagir) → jagirdars; enabled a quick army without a permanent central force.
Q15
Krishnadevaraya (16th c.): expanded & secured the empire over the Deccan; military power + cultural renaissance; patronised Sanskrit, Telugu, Kannada; wrote the Telugu epic Āmuktamālyada; gave grants to temples (Tirupati, Vitthala).
Q16
(1) Shifting the capital to Daulatabad (Devagiri) — people forced to travel 1,000+ km; he later moved it back → great loss of life. (2) Token currency — cheap copper coins declared equal to silver/gold → counterfeiting & confusion → economy declined.

Section D Very Long Answer

Q17
Akbar: early brutality (Chittor massacre) → as he grew in knowledge he rejected forcing his faith; abolished jizya, promoted sulh-i-kul, made Rajput marriage alliances, appointed Hindu officials, held interfaith dialogues, built a "house of translation" → a larger, more stable empire with broad support.
OR — Sikhs: Guru Nanak (15th c.) preached equality & Ik Onkār; persecution forced Guru Hargobind to raise an army; Guru Tegh Bahadur martyred (1675) defending Kashmiri Pandits; Guru Gobind Singh formed the Khalsa (1699); later Maharaja Ranjit Singh unified the confederacies into the Sikh Empire (1799).
