Chapter 1 · India and the World

Natural Resources and Their Use

When does Nature become a resource? How do we sort resources, why is their uneven spread a source of both wealth and war — and how do we use them without using them up?

Big Idea The one-line story of this chapter

Nature exists on its own. The moment humans take something from Nature to use it, that thing becomes a resource. But not everything in Nature is a resource — it must be reachable, affordable and acceptable to use. Because resources are spread unevenly across the Earth, they shape where people live, how they trade, and even why they fight. The chapter ends with a plea: use renewables gently so they regenerate, and use non-renewables wisely so they last — this is stewardship.

Key Concept 1 When does Nature become a "resource"?

Trees, water, air, coal — these are all part of Nature and exist without humans. They become resources only when humans use them for survival or to make something. A tree is Nature; the wooden chair you make from it treats the tree as a resource.

The 3 tests for a resource (very high-yield): for something in Nature to count as a resource it must be —
  • Technologically accessible — we have the technology to reach/extract it.
  • Economically feasible — the cost of extracting it is worth it.
  • Culturally acceptable — society allows its use (e.g. cutting trees in a sacred grove is not acceptable).
Petroleum deep under the ocean that we cannot yet reach is not a resource — until it passes all three tests.
Exam word: here "exploitation" is not negative — it simply means the extraction, utilisation and consumption of natural resources.
Def →Natural resources are materials and substances that occur in Nature and are valuable to humans.
Def →Utility (usefulness) is what makes an object or substance a resource. No utility = not a resource.

Key Concept 2 Two ways to categorise natural resources

We categorise things using shared criteria so we can talk about them with one word. Natural resources are sorted in two important ways:

A. By the use we put them to

CategoryWhat it doesExamples
Resources essential for lifeKeep us alive; we cannot manufacture themAir, water, food (soil)
Resources for materialsTurned into useful or beautiful objectsWood, marble, coal, gold
Resources for energyPower buildings, transport, productionCoal, water, petroleum, gas, sunlight, wind

B. By whether they renew: Renewable vs Non-renewable

RenewableNon-renewable
MeaningNature restores/regenerates them within our timescaleFormed over millions of years; can't be replaced at the rate we use them
ConditionRenewable only if we don't disturb Nature's rhythm (don't harvest faster than it regrows)Will run out — must be used judiciously
ExamplesSolar, wind, flowing water, timber (if regrown)Coal, petroleum, iron, copper, gold
Common trap: A renewable resource can become non-renewable! If we cut timber faster than the forest regrows, or over-fish during spawning season, the resource is depleted and stops renewing. Renewability is a condition, not a permanent label.
Restoration vs Regeneration: Restoration = returning something damaged to its original healthy state (a cut heals, a forest recovers after fire). Regeneration goes further = Nature creates new life and conditions for thriving. Nature works in cycles with no waste.

Key Concept 3 Two "bonus" terms examiners love

Stock →Stock of resources = the total available amount of a resource (whether in use or not), i.e. the whole reserve.
Patent →A patent is the exclusive right over any idea or invention — no one else can use it without permission.
Ecosystem functions vs services: A forest naturally makes oxygen, filters water, prevents soil erosion, gives habitat — these are ecosystem functions. When humans benefit from them (clean water, protected farmland, pollinated crops), they are ecosystem services.
Memorable number: one mature tree makes about 275 litres of oxygen per day, but a human needs about 350 litres per day — so one tree isn't even enough for one person!

Key Concept 4 Uneven distribution — wealth, trade… and conflict

Natural resources are not spread evenly across the planet or within a country. This shapes settlements, trade, international relations and even wars.

  • Benefits: industries near resources create jobs, townships grow, quality of life improves.
  • Costs: people can be displaced from their homes; sacred places get threatened, causing conflict.
  • Shared waters cause tension: Nature ignores political borders. E.g. the Kaveri river is shared (and disputed) by Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Kerala and Puducherry; the Brahmaputra is shared across countries.
The "Natural Resource Curse" (paradox of plenty): having lots of natural resources does not guarantee riches. Countries that can't build industries to convert resources into higher-value products may stay poor. India has largely avoided this curse by investing in industries, technology and skills.

Key Concept 5 Sustainable Development & Stewardship — the heart of the chapter

Sustainable development means meeting our needs today without making it harder for future generations to meet theirs. Irresponsible use has caused pollution, biodiversity loss and climate change.

Principles of Sustainable Development (high-yield 3-marker):
  • Restore & regenerate renewables — don't use them faster than they recover (e.g. recharge groundwater, rejuvenate soil).
  • Use non-renewables judiciously — stretch them until sustainable alternatives are ready; switch to renewable energy.
  • Protect equity — resources like clean water and air must reach all sections of society fairly.
Def →Biodiversity loss = the decline in the variety of life on Earth.
Idea →Stewardship = acting as careful caretakers of Nature. The Bhagavad Gītā's lokasangraha — act for the wellbeing of all, not just personal desire.

Worked Examples Model answers — write them like this

Example A. "What can make what is today a renewable resource non-renewable tomorrow?" [3]
Point 1:A resource is renewable only while its natural rhythm of restoration and regeneration is not disturbed.
Point 2:If humans use it faster than it recovers — e.g. cutting timber faster than the forest regrows, or over-fishing tuna during spawning season — the stock collapses.
Point 3:Prevention: harvest within limits, allow regeneration, replant, and regulate use (like the old ban on fishing during spawning).
∴ Over-use beyond Nature's recovery rate turns a renewable resource into a depleted, non-renewable one.
Example B. "Explain the principles of Sustainable Development." [3]
Idea:Meet present needs without harming future generations' ability to meet theirs.
P1:Restore & regenerate renewables — don't exceed Nature's recovery rate.
P2:Use non-renewables judiciously so they last until greener options arrive.
P3:Ensure fair access to basic resources for all sections of society.
∴ Sustainable development = balance of ecology, economy and equity — stewardship of Nature.
Example C. "What is Stock of Resources?" [1]
Answer:The total available quantity of a resource — the entire reserve, whether currently in use or not.
∴ Stock = total available resources.

Traps & Quick Recall Don't lose easy marks

Iron ore is NON-renewable (a mineral/metal ore), not renewable. Coal & petroleum are non-renewable fossil fuels.
Utility (not beauty, quantity or availability) is what makes something a resource.
  • Coal reserves in India may last only ~50 more years → use judiciously.
  • Renewable examples: solar, wind, flowing water, timber (if regrown). Non-renewable: coal, oil, iron, copper, gold.
  • Resource curse = "paradox of plenty"; India avoided it via industry + skills.
