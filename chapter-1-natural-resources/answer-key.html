Section B Short Answer

Q11
An element of Nature becomes a resource when humans use it. Three conditions: technologically accessible, economically feasible, culturally acceptable.
Q12
Renewable = restored/regenerated within our timescale if not overused (e.g. solar/wind/timber). Non-renewable = formed over millions of years, can't be replaced at our rate of use (e.g. coal/petroleum/iron).
Q13
Ecosystem services = benefits humans receive from Nature's functions — e.g. clean water from a forest that filters it (also pollinated crops, protected farmland).

Section C Long Answer

Q14
Meet present needs without harming future generations. Principles: (1) restore & regenerate renewables, don't exceed recovery rate; (2) use non-renewables judiciously till greener options arrive; (3) ensure fair access to basic resources for all.
Q15
Resource curse / "paradox of plenty" = resource-rich regions can stay poor because they can't convert resources into higher-value products. India avoided it by investing in industries, technology and skills.
Q16
Functions: producing oxygen, filtering water, preventing soil erosion, providing habitat, pollinating crops (any 3). Uneven distribution → competition for scarce resources → displacement, threatened sacred sites, water-sharing disputes → conflict/war.

Section D Very Long Answer

Q17
Punjab: Green Revolution's HYV wheat/paddy needed more water; free power → over-pumping of groundwater; chemical fertilisers/pesticides polluted it. Result: ~80% of Punjab "over-exploited", water below ~30 m, health hazards. Lesson: short-term food security caused long-term unsustainability — use within recovery limits.
OR — Cement: cement is vital but highly polluting (dust harms lungs, leaves, soil, water). CPCB issues guidelines. Sustainable move: revive traditional stone & mud, add plant-based and recycled materials, combine traditional methods with modern technology → less pollution, local jobs, climate-suited buildings.
